DIY Silicon Carbide

I've always been a fan of semiconductors. They're cool. They make modern techonology possible.
Eventually, the question arose: Could I make my own transistors or diodes? The answer is maybe!

Initial Research

Initially I was quite sceptical myself. However, reading about how Point-contact diodes were made back in the day, it gave me hope.
Additionally, consulting the most advanced AI I knew of at the time, Google Gemini (which advised me not to try due to the high temperatures), I figured out what I'd need to make it work.

  • A source of carbon
  • A source of silicon dioxide
  • Lots of heat

    • Thus, my plan was to just stick a pencil lead in some sand and use the resisitve heat of carbon to heat it up.

    First experiments

    My initial attempts were... bad to put it lightly. The sand I used had been extracted from dirt. The carbon rods were pencil leads. Potential success? Probably not. New setup! Lets hope this changes something! Yeah, no...
    Overall, the more I tried, the less it worked, so I reconsidered my approach.

    The Acheson process

    As it turned out, what I was doing had already been discovered and was the primary way to make Silicon Carbide! This process is known as the Acheson process.
    The Acheson process is quite simple, in theory. You take a metal bucket, you put sand in it, put a graphite rod into it, then connect power to both ends of it to generate heat. Get that whole mixture up to around 2000Â°C, and you're making SiC.
    The detail that the Acheson process wikipedia page added was pre-mixing some Carbon into the Silcion Dioxide to improve the chances of the two element reacting.

    Another round

    Another round of experiments. This time, I decided to buy some actual materials. This time, I was equipped with a bucket I got from a hardware store, and some bird sand.
    Even these experiments proved unfruitful. I even tried to use my DIY Power supply, which can pump out more Voltage but the lack of Amps hurt its performance.
    By this point I just started trying whatever I thought may work. I had known about carbon arc lamps before this point, and figured just poking two severed carbon rods may make enough heat to form SiC. After some trial and error, I got this. According to a chart I found online, dark and light green indicate a decent purity.
    Degree of purity (in %) Color
    99.8 light green
    99.5 dark green
    99 black
    < 99 gray
    Whether this chart can be trusted remains to be seen, as I never managed to connect this miniscule piece in any way to test it. Try connecting a cheap Amazon multimeter to essentially a grain of sand!

    So now what?

    I have plans to eventually experiment some more. I even bought myself a Power supply that can output more Amps (but only 750mV). So who knows.
